Paper || Normalization is a general neural mechanism for context-dependent decision making

2020-10-12 16 min read # paper

abstract

sensory systems:

  • divisive normalization in areas ranging from retina to cortex
  • mediating processes: contrast adaptation, surround suppression, visual attention, multi-sensory integration

fruit: choice model using normalization generate significant and irrational choice phenomena driven by either the value or number of alternative options

value-guided choice exp, context-dependent behavior, models with normalization

-> neural mechanism of value coding influences stochastic choice behavior

Neural coding is governed by a small number of canonical computations implemented in diverse circuits and mechanisms

divisive normalization is an example of neural coding, in which the initial input-driven activity of a neuron is divided by the summed activity of a large pool of neighboring neurons, originally proposed to explain nonlinear responses in primary visual cortex.

Recent evidence shows that normalization processes extend beyond sensory areas to higher-order cortical areas involved in decision making.

In parietal and pre-motor cortex, neurons specifying individual actions are strongly modulated by the value of those actions and encoded in a normalized form. Firing rates are increased by increases in the value of the represented action and suppressed by increases in the value of alternative actions

-> context dependence in neural coding

A fundamental assumption of many traditional rational theories of choice is that the context-independent value of options guides decision making instead of the number or quality of other options (independence of irrelevant alternatives)

Here we show that the normalization model predicts significant context-dependent rationality violations in computational simulations, driven by normalized scaling of value-coding neural activity.

In a traditional absolute value representation, relative preference between two fixed target options remains constant as distracter value increases; In a relative value representation, the mean firing rate of each option depends on the values of the other options.

The resulting increase in overlap between firing rate distributions decreases the relative preference of the chooser for the better target option.

But a second type emerges in both value representations: when distracter values approach the target values, increasing the value of the distracter increases the relative preference of the better target option. (driven by choices capture by the distracter option, which selectively competes with the lower-valued target option)

under normalization context dependence is biphasic: effective choice consistently decreases until distracter competition counteracts the effect of divisive scaling and offsets the normalization-induced decrease in relative target preference.

Discussion

predominant example: attribute-based decision (the exact nature of the effect depends on the relative position of the options in multi-dimensional attribute space)

this typically rely on higher cognitive processes such as attentional switching between dimensions or differential weighting of attributes

here: a different mechanism for context dependence arising from the fundamental nature of value representation in the neural circuits; related normalization processes in circuits coding.

In addition to trinary-choice effects, the normalization model predicts a decrement in choice inefficiency with increasing set size; set-size effect can arise directly from the nature of the neural representation of value (although limitations in information processing capacity and potential post-choice dissatisfaction)

In the lateral intraparietal area, neurons represent saccade value in a normalized form, relative to the values of all available saccades.

Reach-related neurons in dorsal pre-motor cortex also encode a relative value representation across potential actions, suggesting a similar value coding in other effector systems.

With fixed rewards, relative value varies monotonically with log likelihood ratio, suggesting that probabilistic information can be implemented via a divisive normalization computation.

Consistent with the predicted effect of set size, LIP neurons recorded during perceptual decision making show lower activity during target presentation and motion viewing with four targets versus two targets, a similar mechanism may underlie the effect of choice set size and target uncertainty in the superior colliculus.

context dependence arises from two critical elements: normalization and stochasticity

  • normalization drives a rescaling of neural activity driven by the value of all choice options

    • the brain also displays normalization to the recent history of rewards

    • temporal normalization can generate context dependence but the consequences of such adaptation in value coding and potential interaction with choice circuit normalization remain currently unknown

    • causality hypothesis: disrupting value normalization should reduce the magnitude of context-dependent choice inefficiency

    • potential candidate mechanisms: feed-forward or feedback inhibition offer attractive targets

  • noise: mediating changing stochasticity as firing rates are scaled by normalization

    • significant effects were observed with both rate-dependent and fixed variability
    • future work: examine the nature of noisy neural codes

in summary, here report a class of anomalous choice behavior predicted by a normalization-based decision model and confirmed in empirical choice data.

context dependence independent of attribute-based choice and contextual set-size effects.
