Paper || Attention biases decision but not alters appearance

作者: LIU Yinuo 日期: 2020-09-21 阅读时间:15.2 分钟 字数:2461 分类: paper

! This is a debate about the previous paper of attention alters appearance

They replicated their study using the exact stimuli and procedures.

They discovered that when we changed the type of decision that subjects performed from a comparative judgment ("which target has higher contrast") to an equality judgment("are the two targets equal in contrast") that is resistant to bias, the reported attentional effects vanished

They also performed a control exp (subjects were instructed on a trial-by-trial basis which judgment to perform only after the stimuli had disappeared) to control the differences in attentional strategies. (?) They find that the magnitude of attentional effect for the comparative judgment was diminished but still significant while the equality measured still no effect.

They conclude that the reported effects of attention upon appearance can be entirely explained by decision bias and that attention does not alter appearance can we conclude that? logically?

maybe we can use neuroscience methods?

Most psychologists in the late 19th and early 20th century thought that attention intensified sensations and suggested that this attentional intensification might be nullified by a compensatory mechanism to preserve the veridical appearance of stimuli.

More recently, electrophysiological recordings in extrastriate cortex - attention increases neural firing rates similarly to increases in stimulus contrast. -> if attention operates by actually increasing stimulus contrast, then without compensation it should manifest in a subjective change in appearance.

But argument.

In this study:

  • subjects were briefly presented with two peripheral Gabor targets of varying contrasts, one of which could be preceded in its spatial vicinity by a transient cue stimulus
  • task the same
  • result: the subjects judged the cued target to have significantly higher contrast than the actual contrast

previous: use different stimuli, solid disks rather than Gabors, unable to measure the effects of attention upon contrast above the detection threshold.

  • To maximize the location uncertainty and therefore the info content of the cue: the targets were presented at random locations within an annulus rather than at two fixed locations.

  • cue effects on the perceived contrast of targets with contrasts near or below the detection threshold (?)

  • these effects were additive over the contrast range rather than multiplicative as would be expected by a contrast gain model of attention, and depended upon the contrast polarity of the cue

conclude: the observed effects of the cues on perceived contrast were likely due to sensory interactions between the cues and targets rather than effects of attention

debate: Ling and Carrasco suggested that perhaps the ring-shaped cues used in Schneider may have invoked meta-contrast masking that exactly canceled the attentional effects. While such a precise cancellation seems unlikely, we decided to try to replicate Carrasco et al, using the same identical spatiotemporal properties and a range of superthreshold target contrasts.

Methods

subjects:

  • 18 (10 male) , avg age of 22 (19-23), paid.
  • three separate sessions in the main exp: 14
  • 8 of the 14 + additional 4: control exp

visual stimuli:

  • fixate (500-1000) - cue (67) - cue-target interval (53) - targets (40) - response

  • 2 or 4 cpd, zero phase and Gaussian envelopes with a standard deviation of 1 degree

  • for the equality and comparative judgments: oriented vertically
  • for the comparative judgment plus orientation: independently and randomly rotated 45 degree clockwise or counterclockwise
  • cue: a 0.3 degree diameter black dot, 1.5 degree directly above the center of one of the two targets

  • contrast of the cued target: 15%. 20%. 25%. 30%. 35%. uncued: chosen from an evenly distributed logarithmic range of contrasts spanning plus or subtract 1 natural log units relative to the cued one, i.e, multiples of 0.36, 0.47, 0.61, 0.77, 1.0, 1.3, 1.6, 2.1, 2.7 (?)
  • overall range of contrasts: 5.5-95%

procedure:

  • each of the 45 combinations of cued/uncued was repeated 20 times (totally 900 trials) (break every fifty trials)

    • comparative judgment: judged which of the two had the higher contrast; press the left or right arrow
    • comparative judgment plus orientation: report the orientation of the target with higher contrast; press s(left-left) d(left-right) k(right-left) l(right-right)

  • no time limit for the responses

  • control exp: the fixation point consisted of the superimposed characters = and +; when the target stimuli disappeared, one of the two characters at the fixation point also disappeared, the the other character remained.

    • the remain one to indicate the judgment to perform; = for the equality and + for the comparative.

data analysis:

  • the equivalent contrasts of the cued targets were determined by fitting the subjects' responses to models through a global maximum likelihood optimization
  • the comparative judgment data were fit to a cumulative normal distribution
  • the equality judgment to another cumulative normal distributions

Locus of the cue effect

difficulty: the cue used to orient attention may have multiple effects

  • low-level sensory interaction with the stimulus
    • through brightness induction or some other mechanism, affecting the registration of the stimuli even at the retinal level
    • e.g. a dark cue reduced the apparent contrast of perithreshold disc targets and a light cue enhanced it
  • orient attention towards the cued, perhaps boosting its apparent contrast in the process
    • potentially altering the appearance of the stimulus & initiate a vatiety of other mechanisms that may prioritize the cued in a task
  • beside the contrast altered, attention could also affect the decision mechanism, prioritizing the cued and causing subjects to tend to more frequently report it as having higher contrast when were perceptually identical
    • attentional selection shortens reaction time, reduces variance, lowers detection thresholds, increases the salience of a target
  • a cue differentiate cued from uncued and could also cause a response bias, independent of attention
    • e.g. motion induction, and changes in temporal order, whose spatiotemporal criteria resemble those required for attention (?)
    • simple response bias

Carrasco: 3 control exp.

  • pose-cue in one
  • demonstrate that their appearance effect depended on the cue lead time in two

-> the result may come from the effect of the cue

  • possible biases in the decision mechanism in three (only choose without orientation detection) this can reveal biases

-> in this: the slopes of the psychometric functions changed, but not eliminate the cuing effect

possibility: a cue could simply increase the salience of the cued target and thereby prioritize it for selection. increasing the contrast also increases its salience, but salience and contrast are not identical properties. a Gabor pattern with a unique orientation among a uniform field will be highly salient compared to its neighbors with subjectively equal contrasts.

the subjects had two judgments to complete without the luxury of prolonged period for information accrual or decision, it would be advantageous to make a quick determination of the relative contrasts in order to move on to the secondary orientation discrimination, especially when they were uncertain.

THUS the focus of this study is to determine whether attentional biasing can explain the results.

signal theory

comparative judgment

The point of subjective equality (PSE), at which the slope of the psychometric function is steepest, is located at the point of of maximum uncertainty, where subjects are most susceptible to influence from even the smallest bit of information that might tip the balance in favor of one of the two targets.

cue may make them always choose the cued one unless they were extremely certain.

equality judgment

If attention actually changes the appearance and increases the perceived contrast of the cued target, then this should cause subjects to be more likely to respond that the cued had an unequal contrast to an identical uncued.

If not actually alter appearance but only serves to prioritize the cued and thereby influence the decision process, then expect that attention would not have the identical effect on the PSE.

Discussion:

if the attentional cues actually changed the appearance of their targets, then the type of judgment performed by the subjects should be inconsequential.

since the cues had no effect in the equality judgment exp, we can eliminate the hypothesis that attention altered the contrast appearance of the stimuli (?)

control exp: attention is affecting the decision process and not perception, could rule out the possibility that predisposition to one judgment or the other could have invoked different perceptual mechanisms. the cues did have significant effect at the lowest two contrast levels for the comparative but no effect for the equality. so the effect of the cues must have been added in the decision process during the comparative but not equality. eliminate the possibility that the lack of an effect of attention upon apparent contrast as measured in the equality could be explained by a different attentional state or strategy employed by the subjects.

explanation for the effects in the comparative and in Carrasco: subjects might have a tendency to respond that a cued target has the higher contrast. Carrasco also performed a control exp (to judge the lower instead of the higher). The bias to associate the cued target with higher contrast must occur during the decision process rather than during the response.

this decision bias seems to be caused by attention because its magnitude is dynamic and depends on the cue lead time similarly to other attentional effects.

attention must operate on the targets through a mechanism that influences the decision process but leaves their appearance intact, e.g. by increasing their salience or the quality or quantity of information about them, or simply flagging them as important.

previous: confound between the potentially biased choice of criterion and the measured PSE. (?)

conclusion

attentional cues may increase the salience and priority of stimuli and thereby, depending on the task demands, incite post-perceptual decision biases.

these processes do not prohibit the veridical perception of sensory attributes.
