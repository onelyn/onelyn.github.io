CONCLUSIONS

  1. when subjects attended to competing, dichoptic stimuli, their conscious perception spontaneously alternated between the two stimuli - one dominant and the other fall, this counterphase modulation is a physiological marker for binocular rivalry

  2. when attention was withdrawn from the competing stimuli, the marker disappeared: binocular rivalry requires visual attention

  3. source localization on the SSVEP topographies shows a dominant source form medial occipital lobe (V1/V2) near the posterior pole and minor contributing sources from bilateral areas near MT

  4. ALL IN ALL, attention is necessary to resolve the interocular conflict in early stages of visual processing

  5. previous studies found that attention could determine the initial dominance, modulate the temporal dynamics of binocular rivalry to some degree and enhance the strength of suppressed signals, but it remained unknown whether binocular rivalry could occur at all when attention was strongly diverted

    • previous: subjects were allowed to attend to the stimuli during brief intervals interspersed with longer periods of unattended rivalry (limited sampling condition) -> infer that perceptual alternation significantly slowed down or even stabilized (rivalry may not occur)
    • our: attention was continuously diverted from the competing stimuli

  6. a previous fMRI study: observe a propagating wave of binocular rivalry resolution even when attention was diverted; the nature of the dynamic change in V1 BOLD signal was ambiguous and could have arisen from other neural events besides the resolution of rivalry

    • using similar stimuli, a more recent fMRI study from the same group failed to find evidence of interocular suppression with dichoptic stimuli when unattended
    • V1 BOLD signal change likely reflected a cross-orientation suppression rather than binocular rivalry
    • frequency-tagging makes identify signals originating from each eye easy

  7. unattended conflicting dichoptic stimuli produced large amplitudes at intermodulation frequencies, more consistent with the neural state produced by binocular fusion than patchwise rivalry

    • interpretation: without attention, rivalry ceases, and the two eyes' signals locally combine in the visual cortex
    • possible mechanism: orientation tuning may broaden without attention, with the consequence being that cells become more permissive in the orientation domain
      • orientation tuning: Neurons in visual cortex are selective for the orientation of a visual stimulus, while the receptive fields of their thalamic input are circular. Cortical orientation selectivity arises from the organization of both thalamic input and local cortical circuits.

In sum, binocular rivalry, seemingly automatic, is in fact highly dependent on attention.
